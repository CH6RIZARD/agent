**What's actually happening here:**

This is a **jurisprudential categorization** for ritual purity rulings (tahara), not an ontological statement about women's nature. Classical fiqh texts organized biological phenomena by shared characteristics relevant to specific legal questions—in this case, which creatures experience menstruation, because that affects rulings about ritual impurity.

**The distinction:**

- **Biological grouping**: "These organisms menstruate" (descriptive fact for legal application)
- **Ontological comparison**: "Women are like animals" (value judgment about essential nature)

The text is doing the former, though the presentation—especially with modern framing like "8 types of animals"—collapses that distinction and makes it sound like the latter.

**The modern controversy:**

Critics argue these classifications—even if "merely" biological—reinforced hierarchical thinking. Defenders say you can't judge pre-modern taxonomy by contemporary standards, and that other parts of Islamic law granted women substantial rights for their era.

The immediate purpose is cataloging which creatures menstruate to determine purity rulings (can you pray after contact with menstrual blood from X animal, etc.). In that narrow sense, it's "studying nature" for legal application.

**2. Deeper level: The taxonomy itself embeds ontological assumptions**

The fact that this classification groups women with animals *at all*—rather than creating a separate category like "humans (both menstruating and non-menstruating)" or addressing women's ritual status independently—reflects underlying frameworks about:

- What categories are "natural" to use
- Which distinctions matter most (biological function > human dignity in this context)
- Who gets centered in legal thought (male as default human, female as biological variant)

**3. The telling detail:**
You'll rarely find classical texts listing "men, bulls, rams, and stallions" when discussing male-specific rulings. The asymmetry reveals that "human + animals with shared biological trait" was considered an acceptable framing *only in one direction*.

**So which is it?**

Both. It's **taxonomic in method** (observing nature to classify) but **ontological in implication** (the choice of taxonomic categories reflects—and reinforces—beliefs about women's fundamental nature relative to men).

The scholars likely didn't see themselves as making a philosophical statement about women's essence. But the framework they used to organize "natural" categories wasn't neutral—it grew from and perpetuated specific hierarchies.

**Modern Islamic feminists argue:** Even granting that pre-modern scholars weren't being deliberately misogynistic, we can recognize how these taxonomic choices shaped centuries of thought about women's spiritual status, and we can reject that framework while maintaining Islamic principles.

Recognizing historical constraints doesn't mean the framework was harmless or should be preserved. The taxonomic choice to group women with livestock for purity rulings—regardless of intent—had real consequences:

- Reinforced women's ritual status as primarily biological/functional
- Made male experience the unstated human norm
- Provided textual ammunition for later restrictions

You can simultaneously:
- Acknowledge these scholars weren't unusual for their time
- Recognize the framework embedded hierarchies
- Reject carrying those hierarchies forward
- Learn from how "objective" categorization often smuggles in power dynamics

**This is why you study primary sources:** You see how seemingly neutral intellectual structures (taxonomy, biology, law) actually construct and naturalize social arrangements. Not to dunk on historical figures, but to understand how categories shape reality.

It's the same analytical move you make with historical analysis generally—understanding how systems functioned without either romanticizing or demonizing the individuals within them.

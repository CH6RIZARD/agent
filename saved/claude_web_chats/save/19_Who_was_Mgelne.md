## ASSISTANT

 Ferdinand Magellan was a Portuguese explorer who led the first expedition to circumnavigate the Earth, sailing under the Spanish flag in 1519.

He set out with five ships and about 270 men to find a western route to the Spice Islands (modern-day Indonesia). He discovered the strait at the southern tip of South America that now bears his name—the Strait of Magellan—and crossed the Pacific Ocean, which he named for its calm waters.

Magellan himself didn't complete the voyage. He was killed in 1521 in the Philippines during the Battle of Mactan, fighting against the local chief Lapu-Lapu. Only one ship, the *Victoria*, and about 18 men made it back to Spain in 1522 under Juan Sebastián Elcano, completing the first circumnavigation.

The expedition proved definitively that the Earth was round and far larger than Europeans had previously estimated—and that the oceans were all connected.

The Nigerian boy in 1519 was just as human, just as capable, just as intelligent as Magellan. The atoms, the physics, the potential—all the same. An iPhone could've theoretically been built by anyone, anywhere, given the right accumulation of knowledge and resources.

What made Europe "ahead" wasn't biological or divine. It was circumstances:

- Geography that encouraged trade and competition between close nations
- Access to certain resources
- Diseases that wiped out their competitors
- Building on knowledge stolen or borrowed from Africa, the Middle East, Asia, and the Americas
- Violence and exploitation that extracted wealth to fund more advancement

If history had gone differently—if the Niger Delta or the Yoruba kingdoms or Great Zimbabwe had certain geographical advantages, or if the Library of Alexandria had survived, or if the Mongols had pushed further, or a thousand other variables—that Nigerian boy's ancestors could've been the ones sailing to "discover" Portugal.

## ASSISTANT

 Realistically? Nah, it wasn't happening.

Not because of ability or intelligence—but because of compounding factors over thousands of years:

**Geography was brutal.** Sub-Saharan Africa had the Sahara Desert cutting it off from Mediterranean trade networks. Europe had coastlines, navigable rivers, and close proximity to other civilizations to exchange ideas with. West Africa had resources but also tropical diseases, rainforests, and less natural harbors for the kind of naval development Europe stumbled into.

**Knowledge compounds.** Europe got lucky inheriting Greek, Roman, Arab, and Chinese innovations—paper, gunpowder, the compass, mathematics. They didn't invent most of it, but they were positioned to absorb and build on it. That accumulation over centuries is what eventually leads to an iPhone. West African societies were developing their own technologies, but the *specific* chain of innovations that leads to electronics, satellites, and microprocessors followed a particular path through particular places.

**Timing.** The Industrial Revolution needed coal, iron, specific economic conditions, and a series of accidents and discoveries that happened to occur in Britain. Hard to replicate that exact sequence elsewhere.

So could a Nigerian empire have *theoretically* built an iPhone given infinite time and different circumstances? Sure. But in the actual timeline, the specific chain of events that led to that technology was locked into a trajectory that started long before Magellan was born.

It's not about capability—it's about path dependency.
